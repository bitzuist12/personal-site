---
title: "The Efficiency Paradox"
date: "2026-04-03"
description: "Why governments are slow in the West but fast in the East — and private markets are the exact opposite."
tags: ["investing", "emerging markets", "observations"]
---

Here's something I noticed after years of doing deals across both developed and emerging markets. There's a paradox that nobody really talks about.

In the West — the U.S., Europe — governments are painfully inefficient. Bureaucracy, regulation, process after process. Getting anything done at the state level feels like pushing a boulder uphill. But private markets? Incredibly efficient. You meet someone, you shake hands, you cut a deal. There's trust. There are contracts that people actually honor. Nobody nitpicks at the last minute. People are long-term oriented — maybe because they're not that hungry, or maybe because the system rewards patience. I haven't fully figured out why, but the result is clear: deal-making in the West is fast, clean, and relatively painless.

Now flip it.

In much of Asia — Vietnam, Indonesia, Turkey, Southeast Asia broadly — governments are remarkably efficient at execution. You can argue the rule of law isn't as developed, and that's exactly what makes them fast. In Vietnam, if the government decides to develop something, they can take your land and move. There's no twenty-year court battle. The state is strong and it acts quickly.

But private market deals? Exhausting.

There's almost zero trust between parties. People are still hungry — understandably — which makes them short-termist. Every clause gets negotiated to death. Nobody believes the other side won't screw them, because frankly, sometimes they do. The contract law infrastructure isn't there to backstop a handshake. So every deal becomes an exercise in protecting yourself from every possible outcome, and that takes forever.

The paradox is this: the places where governments move slowly have private markets that move fast. And the places where governments move fast have private markets that crawl.

Strong state, weak trust between private actors. Weak state (or at least a slow one), strong trust between private actors.

I don't have a grand theory for why this is. Maybe it's that when the state is reliable and slow, people learn to trust each other because they know the courts will back them up. And when the state is powerful but unpredictable, people protect themselves in every transaction because they know nobody else will.

Just an observation. But once you see it, you can't unsee it.
